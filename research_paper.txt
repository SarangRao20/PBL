Q-Module-Bot: A Generative AI-Based Question
and Answer Bot for Module Teaching Support
Mia Allen , Usman Naeem , Senior Member, IEEE, and Sukhpal Singh Gill
Abstract—Contributions: In this article, a generative artiﬁcial
intelligence (AI)-based Q&A system has been developed by
integrating information retrieval and natural language processing
techniques, using course materials as a knowledge base and
facilitating real-time student interaction through a chat interface.
Background: The rise of advanced AI exempliﬁed by ChatGPT
developed by OpenAI, has sparked interest in its application
within higher education. AI has the potential to reshape education
delivery through chatbots and related tools, improving remote
learning and mitigating challenges, such as student isolation
and educator administrative burdens. Yet, ChatGPT’s practical
applications in education remain uncertain, potentially due to
its novel and enigmatic nature. Additionally, current e-learning
chatbot systems often suffer from development complexity and a
lack of input from key stakeholders, leading to developer-focused
solutions rather than user-centered ones.
Intended Outcomes: In this manuscript, we introduce a
practical implementation of AI in education by creating a
system called Q-Module-Bot that is accessible for both technical
and nontechnical educators to harness e-learning beneﬁts and
demystify generative pretraining transformer (GPT).
Application Design: The proposed Q-Module-Bot system has
utilized pretrained large language models (LLMs) to build a
Q&A system that helps students with their queries and supports
education delivery using content extracted from a virtual learning
environment (VLE).
Findings: The prototype and system evaluation conﬁrm the
effectiveness of a scalable cross-departmental tool featuring
source attribution and real-time responses. While successful
in encouraging wider acceptance of GPT use cases in higher
education, reﬁnements are needed for full integration into the
VLE and expansion to other modules/courses.
Index Terms—Artiﬁcial intelligence (AI), chatbots, ChatGPT,
e-learning, generative AI, information retrieval (IR), virtual
learning environment (VLE).

A. Main Contributions
The work in this article presents “Q-Module-Bot,” a tool
that is speciﬁcally designed for educational delivery. The
tool utilizes a knowledge base that is sourced from course
material (namely, the Electronic Engineering and Computer
Science Capstone Project module) that has been scraped
from Queen Mary University of London (QMUL)’s VLE
known as “QMPlus.” The primary objective of this tool is
to address pedagogical challenges encountered in academic
settings. These challenges include limited student engagement
with module content and inadequate feedback loops between
students and educators. As a result, academic responses to
student queries from online messages and emails are often
delayed. Q-Module-Bot has the potential to enhance student
engagement and accessibility while also alleviating the burden
on educators. This system provides actionable insights for
educators, empowering them with the necessary tools to
improve their instructional practices. This includes the ability
to replicate the AI chatbot, thereby enabling customization and
adaptation to individual teaching environments. The system
is the ﬁrst of its kind to utilize a generative pretraining
transformer (GPT) speciﬁcally tailored for taught university
modules and is supported by evaluation data from staff and
students at QMUL. This represents an innovative approach to
enhancing educational quality in the Electronic Engineering
and Computer Science curriculum by leveraging pretrained
large language models (LLMs) for a Question and Answer
(Q&A) system. This system employs information retrieval (IR)
and natural language processing (NLP) methods to efﬁciently
deliver answers, marking a novel implementation of GPT-3
turbo for educational purposes.

II. B
ACKGROUND
This section examines the background of recent develop-
ments in AI and chatbot applications in education, reviews
current initiatives, evaluates accessible technological frame-

A. AI Chatbots and ChatGPT
The development of AI and chatbot technology has evolved
signiﬁcantly since its inception in the 1950s, with a huge
estimated market size of 1.23 billion USD expected by
2025 [8]. Early breakthroughs with chatbots, such as the
creation of the ELIZA Program in the 1960s, marked the
start of an exploration of AI’s potential to emulate and
participate in human conversation, leading to the emergence
of sophisticated chatbots capable of understanding natural
language and responding to complex queries [9]. In the past
year, OpenAI’s ChatGPT has revolutionized the space and
catapulted the technology to a global platform. ChatGPT
utilizes GPT architecture, leveraging NLP and machine learn-
ing (ML) techniques to facilitate seamless and intelligent
conversational interactions with users. Unlike previous AI,
ChatGPT is a generative AI that can produce new content
in real-time conversations. It has the ability to maintain a
persona, allowing for more realistic and engaging dialogues as
a result of training on vast conversational datasets [12].T h e
release of ChatGPT has gained signiﬁcant attention since the
release of GPT-3 and Davinci models in late 2022. ChatGPT
managed to amass 100 million monthly active users in just two
months since its launch, emphasizing the immediate impact
and future potential of the technology [35]. This unprecedented
growth solidiﬁes its position as the fastest-growing online user
application ever recorded with an average of around 13 million
unique visitors engage with ChatGPT daily [20]. With its
remarkable ability to comprehend intricate requests, ChatGPT
is changing the way we engage with technology across the
world, introducing possibilities for enhanced user experiences
as industries explore diverse use cases [9].

chatbots within institutions to support business operations
and efﬁciency and ultimately improve student learning out-
comes [6], [31]. The popularity of platforms and tools which
support online learning is not new, with prepandemic levels
of students enrolled in online or hybrid university courses at
6.7 million in the U.S., and 32% of all university student
enrolments to a fully online course recorded as early as
2012 [15]. It is noteworthy that the recent emergence of
disruptive language models, coupled with their user-friendly
chat interfaces, has had a profound impact on the academic

C. AI Chatbots and ChatGPT
Prior studies on e-learning technology adoption emphasize
the signiﬁcance of forming an online community to enhance
student satisfaction with online learning, as community-
building fosters an environment akin to physical classrooms,
promoting inclusivity and social support among peers and
lecturers, facilitating information exchange and leading to
positive student outcomes [15]. In higher education, learner-
instructor interaction refers to the exchange between lecturers
and students and is relevant to face-to-face and online teaching
approaches. In the context of learner-instructor interaction, the
instructor’s primary responsibilities include igniting student
interest in the course content, motivating learners, and facili-
tating the learning process [6]. Student satisfaction levels in an
online taught university reveal that satisfaction is closely tied
to the fulﬁlment of a student’s interpersonal communication
needs, as they highly value communication and individual,
personal responses as part of their interactions [5].T h i s
is made complex in higher-education settings, where class
sizes can play a crucial role in shaping the dynamics of
academic instruction and student engagement. Smaller class
sizes tend to promote improved and more intimate interactions
between students and educators, fostering stronger rapport.
However, the reality in many universities is that class sizes
are often quite large, with a ratio as high as one student
to tens or hundreds for lecturers, depending on the course
and institution. Consequently, creating meaningful personal
interactions becomes inherently more challenging [4]. Despite
the beneﬁts of student forums and discussion boards, which are
features of many VLEs and for remote courses can facilitate
building relationships and communication channels between
instructor and student [7], they are often under-utilized due
to challenges native to online public forums, such as students
feeling uncomfortable about asking questions in public view
of other students as well as delayed response times [24].
Educational chatbots are gaining popularity as a cost-effective
solution for delivering personalized learning experiences when
it comes to higher education [10] and is arguably one of the
most exciting and engaging tools to promote these experiences
through VLE platforms [11]. Students are highly adept at
using various platforms and receiving responses in real-time,
such as Meta-owned WhatsApp, Facebook, and Instagram
for communication. Chatbots represent a valuable opportunity
to provide university-related information to students instanta-
neously. Such information may include but is not limited to,
assessment criteria, due dates, and other relevant resources.
While various means of interaction exist, such as email,
student forums, and in-person communication, these methods
lack the capability to offer personalized communication that
is readily accessible at all times [4]. Personalization can
drive outcomes that facilitate relationships and trust, improve
student engagement, and support improvements to student
satisfaction with e-learning [7].
D. Chatbots and Supporting Academics
Chatbot systems have the potential to not only enhance
student engagement and provide valuable support to students
but also signiﬁcantly reduce the administrative workload of
academics. By leveraging the capabilities of chatbots, these
systems can effectively automate routine administrative tasks,
such as answering frequently asked questions (FAQs), provid-
ing course materials, and managing student inquiries [4], [11].
This automation liberates academics from time-consuming
administrative responsibilities, granting them more time and
energy to dedicate to crucial aspects of their profession, such
as curriculum development and research. Large class sizes
in university courses can impose signiﬁcant administrative
challenges on lecturers, resulting in limited time to address
student emails and responses to student forums, which may
get lost in overﬂowing inboxes [4].

chatbots rely on predeﬁned developer-set rules, while ML-
based chatbots are more advanced, leveraging AI to understand
both commands and natural language. Rule-based chatbots
have limitations and may fail to comprehend queries that do

F . SWOT Analysis of Existing Applications
A qualitative analysis of chatbots in higher-education
e-learning is analyzed through a SWOTs framework, detailed
in Appendix A in the supplementary material. Adaptability and
learning capabilities, as demonstrated in systems developed
by Sinha et al. [16] and Yang and Stansﬁeld [18], can lead
to improved performance of the bot over time. However,
reliance on predeﬁned responses in rules-based systems, as
observed in Ranoliya et al. [3], may limit handling complex
queries. Overcoming technical complexities highlighted in
Yang and Stansﬁeld [18] and Sinha et al. [16] is crucial for
user-friendly implementation. The precision and accuracy of
responses, reported in systems developed by Sinha et al. [16],
signiﬁcantly impact user satisfaction, suggesting that students
are keen to know the responses provided are accurate. The
emergence of competing solutions, understood by Yang and
Stansﬁeld [18] and Ranoliya et al. [3], poses opportunities
and threats, driving developers to explore novel technolo-
gies. User-centered design and stakeholder input, evident in
Yang and Stansﬁeld [18], are afﬁrmed as a consideration
needed to capture user needs and overall system effectiveness.
However, as observed by Sinha et al. [16] and Feng et al. [8],
neglecting this input may result in mismatches with user
expectations. The analysis emphasizes the importance of stake-
holder input into the design, response precision, and usability
when developing chatbot and Q&A systems for education
purposes. The analysis also highlights the reliance on overly
complex systems, which are not easy to implement, update,
and keep relevant in light of novel technologies and cannot
be utilized by nontechnical audiences. The existing solutions
also lack sufﬁcient relevance for supporting module teaching,
conﬁrming a need for a system to unlock the potential for
enhancing e-learning experiences.

III. S
YSTEM DESIGN METHODOLOGY
Based on the ﬁndings in the literature, corroborated with
user surveys, are the following requirements and user stories.
A. Functional Requirements
1) Q&A Functionality for Academic Support:
User Story: As an educator, I want routine tasks like
addressing FAQs and aiding in module material understanding
to be automated so that I can allocate more time to teaching
students, research and preparing learning materials.
User Story: As a student, I want to be able to ask questions
and receive relevant immediate responses to my queries that
are tailored to my speciﬁc needs, ensuring a seamless and
engaging learning experience.
2) Source Attribution for Credibility:
User Story: As a student, I appreciate transparency in
learning materials. I want the system to provide clear source
attribution to the information it presents, ensuring that I can
trust the authenticity of the content.
3) Adaptability for Cross-Modules and Departments:
User Story: As an educator, I want the system to be
adaptable to different modules and departments so that I can
customize its responses and functionalities according to the
speciﬁc educational needs and requirements of each module.
B. Nonfunctional Requirements
1) User Satisfaction:
User Story: As a student, I expect the system to respond
promptly to my queries, providing personalized answers that
align with my learning goals, ultimately contributing to my
satisfaction with the educational experience.
User Story: As an educator, I want the proposed system
to assist me in managing routine tasks efﬁciently so that I
can focus on creating a rich academic environment, leading to
enhanced satisfaction among my colleagues and students.
2) Stakeholder Conﬁdence:
User Story: As academic support staff, I want to ensure that
the integration of the technology is carried out with educa-
tor oversight, guaranteeing the system’s reliability, accuracy,
and alignment with educational objectives, thereby building
stakeholder conﬁdence.
3) Usability:
User Story: As a user of the tool, I want to interact with
a user-friendly interface, ensuring ease of use and a positive
user experience for students and staff.

D. System Implementation
“Q-Module-bot” is designed to handle two main functions:
1) searching for relevant text chunks in the knowledge base
and 2) generating responses using GPT-3 Turbo. The proposed
system as shown in Fig. 3 has been developed in Python,
which incorporates a data pipeline executed within a Jupyter
Notebook environment, facilitating sourcing module content
via Web-scraping, preprocessing, chunking, tokenization, and
embedding utilizing OpenAI’s Embedding API to generate
dense vector representations of the data and store in a CSV
ﬁle. This component ensures adaptability across modules by
enabling easy integration of new knowledge through changes
made exclusively to the embedding CSV ﬁle. A simple Web
application, developed with Flask serves as the interface for
user interaction and integrates with OpenAI’s API, including
the Q&A component powered by GPT-3.5 Turbo. This compo-
nent handles user queries, generates responses, and facilitates
natural language interaction.
E. Methodology
Fig. 4 shows the prototype search screen. The following are
the main components.
1) Web-Scraping: This has been done by utilizing Beautiful
Soup and Selenium, Python libraries used for Web
scraping, which allows the system to extract module data
from QMUL’s VLE.
Fig. 5. Prototype Q&A bot response screen.
2) Preprocessing: Pandas and Matplotlib preprocess the
data, removing noise, tokenizing, and formatting it into
a CSV ﬁle.
3) Embedding: OpenAI’s embeddings API transforms the
tokenized data into dense vector representations, which
are then stored in a local CSV ﬁle for use within the
Web application.
4) Application: Flask’s Web framework serves as the main
interface of the application and loads data from a
CSV ﬁle containing embeddings generated from text
chunks. These embeddings are then preprocessed and
converted from string representations to NumPy arrays,
ensuring efﬁcient computation. To search for relevant
text chunks, the application implements a sophisticated
ranking system based on the relatedness of the query to
the content in the knowledge base; this process involves
utilizing spatial distance computations to determine the
similarity between embeddings. The application formu-
lates queries for GPT-3.5 Turbo to generate responses,
incorporating relevant source texts from the knowledge
base (Fig. 5). When a user submits a query, the Web
application constructs a tailored message for the GPT

IV . S
ERVICE EVA L UAT I O N
To validate the system against the requirements and analyze
the effectiveness of the system, a service evaluation was
conducted with ﬁve academic stakeholders from the initial
stakeholder group and ﬁve students from the M.Sc. project
module used to test the system, which was deployed on
Heroku. The group was asked to test the system by asking
questions of their choice about this module. The participants
were asked to anonymously answer a short questionnaire with
the option for plain text feedback. The survey was created to
reﬂect the initial requirements, with each question validating
an initial requirement. A detailed breakdown of the results
is presented in Appendix E in the supplementary material.
The results were overwhelmingly positive; as seen in Fig. 6,
90% of users either strongly agreed or agreed that “Q-Module-
Bot” was easy to use, 100% of users either strongly agreed or
agreed that the text chunks and relatedness scores increased
their conﬁdence in the system, as reﬂected in Fig. 7, 100% of
users either strongly agreed or agreed that the system could be
extended to other modules and 100% of users either strongly
agreed or agreed that the responses were relevant and helpful.

Noteworthy feedback emerged from the service evaluation
that will contribute to future enhancements. In terms of user
feedback, evaluators noted that the “relatedness score” was too
lengthy and suggested minor user interface adjustments, such
as optimization of font sizes for responses and conﬁguration

interface and full integration within the VLE system would
enhance accessibility and user experience, further optimizing
its effectiveness in educational settings. Q-Module-Bot, like

originating from the quality of input data, as the training data
used for the module may be subjective. This is something that
will be explored extensively in future iterations of this tool.

F
UTURE WORK
There are ample opportunities for reﬁning and enhancing
the functionality of “Q-Module-Bot” based on user feed-
back. The initial user studies shed light on technical glitches
within existing systems, highlighted by 60% of academics,
reinforcing the need for robust and dependable technological
solutions. This underscores the importance of comprehensive
testing to ensure reliability and mitigate any potential points
of failure before considering implementation in university
systems. Additionally, several avenues for future enhance-
ments are evident, addressing existing shortcomings and
catering to speciﬁc needs, including the addition of training
materials to the knowledge base. One promising enhancement
involves enabling the system to handle more intricate question
types that revolve around technical problem-solving, integrat-
ing the system into assessment scenarios holds signiﬁcant
potential, extending its utility beyond its current scope [36].
Furthermore, work could be done to only include text chunks
in the response formulation if over a speciﬁc “relatedness”
threshold (e.g., if over 80% conﬁdence).
A. Advanced Generative AI Models
The long term objective is that Q-Module-Bot will be
updated regularly with the latest Generative AI models
to improve the performance in terms of accuracy of the
responses generated, which will be validated using precision
and recall [38].
B. Experiments With Larger Sample Size
The true potential of the system will become clear when
it is used as an integral part of university module teach-
ing throughout an academic term. Beyond its immediate
beneﬁts as a Q&A system, an exciting prospect emerges—
leveraging the wealth of data it generates for deeper insights.
By analyzing this data, we can not only identify areas of
content that students ﬁnd most challenging but also gain
a nuanced understanding of their engagement levels [26].
This data-driven approach serves as a valuable metric for the
overall health of modules and ultimately academic institutions,
offering actionable insights to inform decision-making and
targeted teaching service interventions. These insights could
empower educators and administrators to proactively address
issues, reﬁne teaching strategies, and optimize the learning
experience. The journey ahead holds transformative possibil-
ities for educational technology. By embracing analytics and
using the system as a catalyst for meaningful change, we pave
the way for a future where education is continually enhanced
through innovative solutions and evidence-based approaches.
In terms of the service evaluation, a limitation we encoun-
tered was related to the small sample size of the stakeholders
involved. To address this issue, we intend to employ interpreta-
tive research inquiry in the form of semi-structured interviews
with the stakeholders [37]. By doing so, we aim to explore
